# Top Gaps — Evidence Memo v1 Prioritization (ranked)

1. **[Internal contradiction — threatens the #1 ranking] Usage score for Study & Memory is built by double-counting.** The Usage=4 rests on "explaining concepts" prevalence (58–80%), but Section 2 classifies explanation-seeking as the passive, low-utility behavior the product must redirect *away* from. Usage of the actual target behavior (retrieval/self-quizzing) is 1.8–11%. Scored on the behavior the product sells, Usage ≈ 1–2 and Study & Memory (≈3.4–3.8) drops to or below Reading & Research. No sensitivity analysis on this rescoring is presented, despite Usage carrying the largest weight (0.40).

2. **[Population not covered] The first user base is absent from the evidence.** Every prevalence figure is UK (HEPI), US (Pew, Middlebury, Anthropic, Tyton), German, or global-panel; zero Romanian/CEE usage, discipline-mix, device, or language data beyond a self-flagged "thin, IT-skewed" note. Worse: **no primary research at all** — not one interview, usability session, or survey of a prospective user. A prioritization memo with a 0.40 usage weight anchored entirely on other countries' students, and an unexploited Figshare dataset the memo itself identifies, is a first-order gap.

3. **[Unverified transfer of the load-bearing causal claim] Bastani et al. is Turkish *high-school math* students.** The memo's cornerstone triad (crutch harm, guardrail cure, perception gap) — cited "verified" seven times and underpinning design patterns 3 and 5 — is extrapolated to university, mixed-discipline, mixed-language users without flagging the population jump. Same issue for Kestin et al. (Harvard physics, one course) supporting Impact=5. Verification checked the *papers*, not the *transferability*.

4. **[Load-bearing claims unverified] All three Differentiation-5 scores rest on unverified vendor pages and review blogs.** GAP 1–3 (persistence, pipelines, toggles) drive the top and third rankings via a 0.30-weight criterion, are self-labeled "moderate, unverified," reflect a mid-2025 snapshot (Quizlet Q-Chat, ChatGPT Tasks caps, Notion pricing), and include no incumbent-roadmap horizon scan. One OpenAI or Google release (e.g., persistent mastery state in Study Mode/NotebookLM) erases GAP 1 and the memo has no monitoring or contingency for it.

5. **[Untested bridging assumption] AI-generated retrieval items ≠ the items in the cited science.** Every effect size (Roediger & Karpicke, Cepeda, Latimier, Rowland) used experimenter- or human-authored test items. The core loop ("every summary emits a question deck") assumes auto-generated items match that quality; the literature on LLM-generated question validity, difficulty calibration, and hallucinated flashcards is not searched at all. The memo notes the missing *combined* RCT but misses this narrower, checkable component claim.

6. **[Counter-evidence unexplored] Boundary conditions on the testing effect.** Impact=5 presents retrieval practice as unconditional, but known moderators cut against the use case: attenuation with high element-interactivity/complex materials (van Gog & Sweller 2015 line), weaker transfer to higher-order university tasks, and most spacing studies using verbatim/factual recall. For law essays, proofs, or clinical reasoning, g≈0.50 may not hold — unaddressed.

7. **[Modality not searched] EU regulatory and privacy exposure — for a Romania-based builder.** Mastery ledgers, error logs, per-course performance histories, and the recommended "first-cohort telemetry" are personal data under GDPR, and education-related AI sits in the EU AI Act's high-risk orbit. Zero coverage of consent, data residency, or whether "human-readable memory files in the user's repo" is a compliance feature or liability. This can constrain the architecture the whole memo recommends.

8. **[Counter-evidence unexplored + internal tension] Adherence to friction-by-default is assumed, not evidenced.** The memo attacks incumbents' bypassable toggles (GAP 3), then designs a bypassable default ("answer always reachable") — functionally a per-interaction toggle, with its own evidence (67% ask for the answer first; 50% would circumvent bans) predicting bypass. No search of the desirable-difficulties abandonment literature, spaced-repetition dropout rates (the 82.1% Anki-overwhelm stat is cited as a competitor weakness, not as a warning about its own core loop), or engagement-mechanics evidence. The end-metric ("bypass rate") admits the thesis is untested; no prior evidence was sought.

9. **[Internal contradiction] Uneven evidentiary standards across jobs.** Planning is explicitly docked one point for correlational evidence (Aeon r=.262), while Study & Memory's Impact=5 leans on the equally correlational, self-selected UCF Anki cohort (6.2–12.9%) with no dock and no confound caveat. Applying the planning standard symmetrically weakens the #1 job's headline score.

10. **[Populations not covered — within the cohort] Graduate/Master's students, working and part-time students, students with disabilities, and non-English study contexts.** Nearly all surveys are undergraduate; ADHD/dyslexia populations (for whom spacing and implementation intentions have distinct evidence and high product relevance) are unexamined; and quiz/explanation quality in Romanian-language coursework — plus mobile-first study habits vs a desktop/terminal power tier — is never assessed despite "AI-novice, mixed-discipline" being the stated target.

11. **[Counter-evidence unexplored] Help-seeking is social.** The memo's own Tyton citation (84% turn to people first, 17% to GenAI) is used only to lower Planning's usage score, never confronted as a challenge to the entire solo-tool thesis; peer/study-group modalities and evidence on collaborative study effects are absent from both the evidence search and the design section.

12. **[Load-bearing assumption unexamined] Both tiers presuppose Claude access in a cohort where 11.3% pay for anything.** The memo flags missing willingness-to-pay data but not the sharper version: the "graceful free-tier degradation" strategy depends on unverified assumptions about claude.ai free-tier limits sustaining a daily retrieval loop, and the power tier requires a paid Claude Code subscription — a structural adoption ceiling never priced into the Differentiation or Usage scores.
