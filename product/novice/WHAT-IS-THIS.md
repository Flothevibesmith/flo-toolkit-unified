# Flo's Toolkit — what it is, in 2 minutes

## What it is

Not an app. A **carefully written instruction sheet you paste into a Claude Project
once** (10-minute setup, laptop). From then on, every chat inside that Project talks
to a Claude that behaves like a personal study coach instead of a generic chatbot.
You already pay for Claude — this makes it actually work for uni.

## The problem it fixes

Three things go wrong when students use AI normally: every chat starts from zero so
you re-upload the same PDFs forever; Claude sometimes makes things up and nobody
checks; and summaries come out generic because the question was vague. The toolkit
packages the skill into the instructions — you can keep asking vague questions and
still get good results.

## How it works — the two tricks

**Trick one: memory.** Claude has a memory feature (you switch it on once during
setup — it's per-Project and you can see and edit everything it remembers in your
settings). The toolkit is designed to feed it: every study session ends with a short
progress recap — your courses, exam dates, weak spots, what's due — so the next
conversation picks up exactly where you left off. **You never save anything.** No
more re-uploading, no more starting from zero.

**Trick two: it fixes your questions before answering them.** Ask something vague
("help me with econ") and it fires back a quick tap-through — 6–10 multiple-choice
questions (answer like "1b 2a", or just say "defaults") — then shows you the precise
request it built from your answers and asks: run it, change it, or just answer the
original? Vague question in, sharp answer out — and you slowly learn what a good
question looks like by seeing yours get rebuilt.

## What you can say (five commands, English or Romanian, natural phrasing)

- **"explain this to me"** — a proper explanation, plus one 15-second recall question
  at the end so it sticks.
- **"read this for me"** — drop slides/PDFs, get structured notes *plus* quiz
  questions ranked by importance. Then it asks you to explain one idea back —
  that's what makes it stay in your head.
- **"quiz me"** — a 5-minute quiz. Real recall questions, one at a time; it asks how
  confident you are *before* showing the answer; you grade yourself. Keeps you at
  ~80% success and targets your weak topics.
- **"make my exam kit"** — the big one. First tells you *what's missing* from your
  uploads, then builds: a topic map ranked by exam likelihood (with reasons), a
  complete question deck, and a day-by-day countdown plan.
- **"review my draft"** — honest essay feedback (argument, structure, evidence,
  clarity) with 2–3 concrete fixes. It won't rewrite it for you unless you ask.

## Built-in protections you never have to think about

- **Everything is sourced.** Claims from your materials point to the exact file and
  slide; outside knowledge gets labeled "outside your materials". You're protected
  from hallucinations without knowing the word.
- **No fake progress.** Quizzing something you learned 5 minutes ago counts as
  practice — only correct answers *a day or more later* count as mastery. That's the
  difference between feeling ready and being ready.
- **Quota awareness.** Before heavy jobs: "this will eat quota — want the lighter
  version?"
- **Phone mode.** Quiz on the bus — same account, it remembers automatically.
- **Shareable decks.** Question decks paste cleanly into the group chat; your
  personal progress file never does.
- **Self-repair.** Anything feels off → type **"is it working?"** and it diagnoses
  itself.

## Why it's built this way

Fifty years of learning research says one thing clearly: **testing yourself beats
rereading, and spacing it out beats cramming** — it's just miserable to organize by
hand. The toolkit makes it happen as a side effect of things you'd do anyway: ask for
a summary, you *also* get a deck; come back tomorrow, it *already knows* what to quiz
you on.
